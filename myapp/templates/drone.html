AI Drone Tag · Interactive brief

Teaching a drone to catch a moving target

A flight pilot that's learned, not programmed — trained entirely in simulation, with a control interface built to fly real hardware.

39-D observation 4-D action 100 Hz control 256×256 policy PPO model-free

Catching a drone that doesn't want to be caught is a control problem that resists hand-written rules. A target that weaves, brakes, and breaks sideways the moment it senses a threat will beat any fixed "if it goes left, follow left" logic. AI Drone Tag takes the other route: the interceptor's pilot is a neural network that learns to chase, discovered through reward and repetition rather than written by hand. It trains entirely in simulation — millions of chases — and its output is the same command a real flight controller already speaks, so the learned pilot is built from the start to leave the sim and fly real hardware.

01 The system

One loop, run a million times

Every bit of behaviour comes out of a single closed loop. The drone senses its situation as a vector of numbers, a compact network turns that into a flight command, the simulated chase plays out the physics against an evading target, and a reward signal quietly tunes the network toward catching it. Run that loop enough and a pilot emerges — no chase logic was ever written, only a definition of what "good" looks like.

02 The curriculum

Learning to fly, then to hunt

You can't put a fresh network in front of a tactical, full-speed evader and expect it to learn — it would fail every episode and pick up nothing. So the task is split into three blocks of rising difficulty. Tap a block to see its real training phases.

03 Earning progression

Mastery, not a schedule

Progress through the curriculum is earned. Before reinforcement learning even begins, the pilot is warm-started by imitating a simple scripted expert — but only on flying, never on the chase, so the hunting is always learned. A mastery controller then holds the agent in each stage and advances it only once its rolling success rate clears the stage's gate. Hover a band for what it trains.

FIG 03 — every stage is a gate; the agent only advances by clearing it.

04 Inside one step

A hundredth of a second

Underneath, one tick of the pilot is small and fast: read the true state, pack it into a 39-number vector, run a compact two-layer network, and read out four numbers that decode into collective thrust and body rates — the low-level language of any flight controller. Run a tick to watch it flow.

39 in → 4 out, at 100 Hz

05 The control abstraction

Above the mixer

A natural question: are those four numbers commands to the four motors? They aren't — and that's the most important design choice in the project. The pilot never touches individual motors. It outputs one collective thrust and three rotation rates for the drone as a whole, and a separate, fixed piece of flight-controller firmware — the mixer — works out the four individual motor speeds that produce that thrust and those rotations.

FIG 05 — the 4-number command is the only interface; everything in the dashed box is existing hardware.

That seam is why this transfers to real hardware. If the pilot commanded raw motor speeds, it would be married to one airframe's motor count, geometry, and thrust curves, and it would have to learn low-level stabilisation from scratch. By stopping at thrust-and-body-rates, it learns what manoeuvre to fly and lets the drone's existing rate controller and mixer handle how to spin the motors — the same interface every modern flight stack (Betaflight, PX4 in rate mode) already exposes. The four numbers are identical in simulation and on hardware; only the physics below the seam changes.

06 Borrowing from the champions

The sim-to-real playbook

The reference point for this whole approach is Swift, the UZH system that beat human champions at FPV drone racing using a network trained entirely in simulation. It's the proof that a policy learned in a sim can fly aggressive, real-world manoeuvres — and it used the same collective-thrust-and-body-rates interface this project does. The lesson worth stealing isn't the racing; it's how they closed the gap between sim and reality, because a policy that wins in a perfect simulator and falls out of the sky in the real world is worthless.

Three moves from that playbook map directly onto this project. First, don't chase a perfect physics model — measure the real drone, then randomise mass, thrust, motor lag and latency across ranges that bracket reality, so the real drone lives inside the distribution the pilot trained on. Second, model the cheap killers early: control and sensing latency, and motor/ESC lag are what usually break transfer, not exotic aerodynamics. Third, learn a residual — fit the leftover gap between sim and real flight logs and add it back into the simulator, rather than trying to derive every force from first principles.

This is exactly what the project's fidelity ladder (F0–F6) is built to absorb, in impact order: rigid-body system identification, then actuator dynamics, then latency, then drag. One more borrow for later: an asymmetric setup, where a privileged critic sees the true world state during training while the pilot only ever sees the realistic, noisy observation it will have on hardware. The heuristic expert already reads that privileged state — the same trick can sharpen the policy without ever leaking information the real drone won't have.

07 Why it matters

The market is converging on the behaviour layer

This is not a weapons project. It's a research effort into autonomous pursuit — the software that lets an aircraft reach a moving, evasive target on its own. But it's striking how directly the defence world is converging on the same problem. In June 2026, the Lithuanian firm Granta Autonomy unveiled the Black Wasp, an autonomous interceptor whose entire value proposition is the guidance: its makers say the AI flight-control system — automated flight, target tracking, terminal guidance without a human flying each second — is where they concentrated their most significant engineering investment.

That's the whole thesis in someone else's press release. The airframe is a commodity; the hard, valuable, defensible part is the autonomous behaviour layer — the pilot that decides how to fly. The established names in counter-drone (Anduril, RTX, DroneShield and the rest) are largely hardware and effector companies; the intelligence that sits on top, hardware-agnostic and improving with every training run, is the part nobody owns yet. A simulator that produces transferable autonomous pilots is a wedge into exactly that layer.

So the interesting takeaway isn't "build a weapon." It's that the part everyone is racing toward — learned autonomy that survives contact with a real, evading target — is precisely the part this project is built to produce, in software, on commodity hardware, improving by iteration rather than by manufacturing.

From a definition of "good" to a pilot that hunts — learned in simulation, built to fly.
